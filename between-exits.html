Issue Zero · Photo Essay

Between
Exits

Ikebukuro and Shinjuku exist in a permanent state of arriving, leaving, and working out who goes first.

Photography and words by Eastokyo · Tokyo, 2026

Ikebukuro and Shinjuku are usually described by scale: passenger numbers, station size, crowds, signs, exits. That tells you how busy they are, but not what it feels like to move through them.

The smaller moments are more useful. A taxi waiting under a signal. Someone stopping at the edge of a crossing to check a phone. A delivery trolley cutting across the pavement. The half-second pause before a ticket gate accepts a card and lets the next person through.

These photographs follow that movement from street level to the corridors below. They are not an attempt to explain either district in full. They are a record of what happens between one destination and the next.

01 · Street level

The city as interface.

Outside Ikebukuro Station, directions compete for attention. Exit numbers, bus stops, shop signs, road markings, and pedestrian signals all sit inside the same field of view. Regulars barely look. Everyone else checks, corrects course, and checks again.

The crossing gathers people in loose rows. When the light changes, those rows break apart immediately. Commuters, shoppers, cyclists, delivery workers, and people waiting for somebody all enter the same space with different plans.

From a distance the movement can look organised. At ground level it is much messier, and more interesting. The street runs on quick judgement: speed up, step aside, leave a gap, take it before it closes.

Shinjuku pushes the same logic harder. Signs pull the eye upward while station entrances pull people below the road. The district appears to offer endless choice, but every route is shaped by rails, arrows, barriers, and the position of the next staircase.

A photograph slows this down enough to show the pattern. One person becomes visible inside the crowd. A traffic light, a building edge, or a small gap between strangers suddenly matters. The noise is gone, but the structure remains.

02 · Below street level

Light, footsteps, and a little anonymity.

Underground, distance works differently. A turn, a staircase, a wall of signs, then another turn. Street names matter less than exit numbers. The weather disappears until the next opening to the outside.

People who know the route move without hesitation. Others slow down near junctions and try not to become an obstacle. The etiquette is simple and learned quickly: keep moving, step aside before stopping, read the sign without blocking everybody behind you.

For a few seconds, bright openings turn people into silhouettes. Coats, bags, and gestures replace faces. There is anonymity in that, but not necessarily loneliness. Strangers share a narrow space and ask almost nothing of one another.

These passages are often treated as empty space between proper destinations. They are not empty. Cleaners start shifts here. Parents steer children through crowds. Musicians carry instruments. Office workers adjust their pace to make a connection. The corridor has its own daily life, even if nobody plans to stay.

03 · Through the gates

The journey just keeps going.

Ticket gates reduce travel to a familiar sequence: approach, tap, beep, step. Most people complete it without thinking. A different tone stops the flow at once and produces the usual brief search for the right card or fare.

The gates are a boundary, but they are also a rhythm. They take in one journey and release the next. Beyond them, routes divide again: another line, another platform, another exit, another street.

That may be the most honest thing about these station districts. Arrival rarely means stopping. The city keeps handing you another direction.

Between exits, private plans meet shared infrastructure. People adjust, hesitate, hurry, and make room. None of it looks remarkable for very long. That is exactly why it is worth photographing.
