Issue Zero · Photo Essay

Between
Exits

Ikebukuro and Shinjuku are places of arrival, departure, and constant negotiation. A photographic essay about the city’s quieter movements above and below street level.

Photography and words by Eastokyo · Tokyo, 2026

Tokyo’s major stations are often introduced through numbers: millions of passengers, dozens of exits, platforms stacked above and below one another, entire neighborhoods organized around the daily pressure of arrival and departure. The figures are impressive, but they do not explain what it feels like to move through these places.

At street level, the city is read in fragments. A signal changes. A taxi pulls forward. A delivery cart crosses the pavement. Someone stops beneath a sign and turns their phone toward the station map. People who have never met adjust their paths around one another without discussion. The system works through thousands of tiny negotiations, most of them completed in seconds.

Ikebukuro and Shinjuku reward this kind of attention. Both are famous for scale, commerce, entertainment, and crowds, but neither exists as a single image. They are collections of thresholds: station doors, crossings, staircases, underground corridors, ticket gates, and exits that divide the surrounding streets into mental territories. To say “meet me at the station” is not enough. The city demands a number, a direction, a landmark, a side.

These photographs follow part of that choreography. They do not attempt to document every district or explain every route. They stay with a few moments when movement becomes visible: a pause before traffic resumes, the temporary room made by a crossing, the compression of an underpass, the repeated gesture of passing through a gate.

01 · Street level

The city as interface.

Outside Ikebukuro Station, information competes for the eye. Building names, advertisements, road markings, bus stops, pedestrian signals, storefronts, and station entrances all ask to be read at once. People learn to filter the city quickly. A familiar route becomes almost automatic; an unfamiliar one requires constant correction.

The east exit opens into a broad field of movement. Traffic turns across pedestrian routes. Shoppers gather and separate. Bicycles appear from the edge of the frame. People wait at the curb in loose rows, then spread across the road when the signal changes. For a brief interval, the crossing belongs to pedestrians. Then the pattern reverses.

A large crossing is a temporary public room with no walls. Everyone enters at once, yet almost nobody stays. Direction matters more than identity. The crowd is not a single body but an accumulation of private intentions: work, shopping, an appointment, a train connection, a meal, a return home. Seen from a distance, the movement can look synchronized. At ground level, it is full of hesitation, acceleration, and small acts of courtesy.

Shinjuku intensifies this feeling. Signs rise above the street while entrances pull people downward. The district seems to offer endless choice, but movement is constantly being directed: this lane, that staircase, this side of the barrier, that passage under the road. The city presents itself as freedom and instruction at the same time.

Photography slows the sequence. A person who would normally disappear into the crowd remains inside the frame. A signal, vehicle, or building edge becomes part of a temporary composition. The photograph cannot reproduce the noise or pressure of the street, but it can reveal the structure beneath it: bodies arranging themselves around lines, gaps, and permissions.

02 · Below street level

Light, footsteps, anonymity.

Below the crossings, Tokyo changes scale. The sky disappears. Distance is measured by turns, signs, stairs, and the number of corridors still ahead. Street names matter less than exit numbers. The city becomes an interior landscape.

Underground routes are designed for flow, yet they also produce uncertainty. A sign can confirm that the destination is close while revealing three more choices. Commuters move with the confidence of repetition; visitors slow near junctions and risk becoming islands in the stream. Everyone learns the etiquette quickly: keep moving, step aside before stopping, read without blocking the route.

In an underpass, people are often seen first as silhouettes. Bright openings flatten individual details, turning coats, bags, and gestures into moving shapes. Anonymity is not necessarily loneliness here. It can be practical, even generous. Strangers share limited space without demanding recognition from one another.

The sound of footsteps changes underground. So does time. A route that takes five minutes can feel longer when there is no horizon, or shorter when the crowd carries everyone forward. Fluorescent light removes the weather. Rain, heat, and evening remain outside until the next staircase.

These corridors are usually treated as spaces between meaningful places. Yet they contain their own daily life: cleaners beginning a shift, staff giving directions, musicians carrying instruments, parents guiding children through crowds, office workers adjusting pace to catch a connection. The passage is not empty simply because nobody intends to remain there.

03 · Through the gates

Every journey continues.

Ticket gates reduce travel to a repeated sequence: approach, tap, tone, step. The action is so familiar that the body completes it before the mind pays attention. A successful sound releases the passenger forward. A different tone creates an instant obstruction, a brief apology, a search for the correct card or fare.

The gates are boundaries, but they are also instruments of rhythm. They divide paid space from the rest of the city while processing people continuously in both directions. During busy periods, the movement feels almost mechanical. Yet every passage still belongs to an individual journey.

Beyond the gates, routes separate again. A platform, another line, an exit, a street. Arrival rarely means stopping. In station districts, each destination becomes the beginning of another sequence.

This may be the defining movement of Tokyo: not speed alone, but continuation. The city receives interruption and absorbs it. Crowds form and dissolve. Construction redirects familiar paths. Stations open into neighborhoods that change character within a few blocks. Everyone keeps adjusting.

Between exits, the city is neither origin nor destination. It is the active space where plans meet infrastructure, where private time enters a shared system, and where ordinary movement becomes the visible life of the street.
