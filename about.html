About Eastokyo

Close
Attention

A photography-led magazine about the people, routines, and overlooked corners that make Tokyo feel lived in rather than merely visited.

Not a guidebook. Definitely not a postcard.

Eastokyo is an independent editorial project made in Tokyo. It starts with photographs, then stays with them long enough to notice what is actually there: movement, work, waiting, weather, and the small negotiations that fill an ordinary day.

The name is not a border on a map. It is an angle of view—looking outward from the centre, toward routes and neighbourhoods people often hurry through without properly seeing.

How the magazine works.

Specific places. Original photography. Writing that does not sound like a tourism pamphlet. No invented reporting.

01

Stay local

Every story starts with a real street, station, shop, room, or person—not a generic idea of Japan borrowed from a stock photograph.

02

Look slowly

Ordinary things become interesting when somebody stops long enough to pay attention: a crossing, a clock, a barrier, a staircase going nowhere remarkable.

03

Show the work

The photographs lead. Captions, sequencing, and context are what turn a folder of images into a story worth reading.

Issue Zero: Tokyo, currently in motion.

This first issue follows the churn of Ikebukuro and Shinjuku, then slows down for four smaller observations: a staircase, a construction mascot, a public clock, and infrastructure that refuses to look finished.

Issue Zero is a first edition and a promise of what comes next: more photo essays, field notes, neighbourhood stories, portraits, and an ongoing record of a city that keeps changing its mind.
