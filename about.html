About Eastokyo

Not Here
to Behave

Eastokyo is an independent magazine about the real Tokyo, not the Tokyo people think they know because they’re into Pokémon or Hello Kitty. We’ll be the first to admit we like sushi, matcha, ninjas, and geishas as much as anyone else. But everyone already knows about that stuff, and it isn’t really what Tokyo’s about. That’s just the mask Tokyo wears in public. We’re here to show you the unmasked version.

This is not your typical magazine, and we’re not here to sell you the illusion that Tokyo has its shit together, because it doesn’t.

Eastokyo is written and photographed by James Simmons, along with the occasional guest author. We’re not a magazine that hypes up Tokyo with stories about fat sumo wrestlers, breathtaking Mount Fuji sunsets, or traditional green tea ceremonies. Tokyo is a megacity with real problems, just like any other major city in the world. The only difference is that people here are expected to keep their mouths shut all the time, which makes it a lot easier to pass Tokyo off as an ideal society without complaints.

Everything is supposed to operate at the highest levels of efficiency, 24 hours a day, because of the strict social order and unspoken standards. That version of reality is based on a true story, but it’s mostly exaggerated. Japanese people are people, just like anyone else in the world. The big difference is that they have been told to shut up so often that they just don’t know how to speak up. We’re here to help them find their voice and tell you how it really is in Tokyo.

How We Do Things

Original photography and real articles. This is not another recycled “look how cool Japan is” magazine or tourism board spin pretending everything is simply marvelous.

The August Issue: Clean on the Surface

The first full issue looks at the gap between Tokyo’s carefully guarded public image and the realities behind the mask. Seven stories uncover social appearances, foreign workers, loneliness, belonging, workplace culture, enforced conformity, and the myth of the submissive Japanese woman.

The stories are written and photographed by James Simmons in Tokyo, Japan, with the courage and dedication to tell you what’s really going on instead of giving you some Camelot version of the city.
